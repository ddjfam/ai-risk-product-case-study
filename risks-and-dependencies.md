# Risks and Dependencies

## Overview

This document outlines example risks and dependencies for an AI risk workflow product. In enterprise environments, successful delivery depends not only on strong product design, but also on stakeholder alignment, operational readiness, governance clarity, and tool/process integration.

## Key Risks

### 1. Stakeholder Misalignment
Different stakeholder groups may have different expectations for workflow design, priorities, controls, and success measures.

**Potential impact**
- slower decisions
- conflicting requirements
- roadmap delays
- lower adoption

**Mitigation ideas**
- align early on goals and scope
- define decision-makers clearly
- document prioritization criteria
- review tradeoffs regularly

### 2. Unclear Requirements
If requirements are incomplete or inconsistent, teams may build the wrong features or miss critical workflow needs.

**Potential impact**
- rework
- delivery delays
- poor user experience
- low confidence in workflow outputs

**Mitigation ideas**
- validate requirements through stakeholder review
- use structured intake and discovery
- write clear user stories and acceptance criteria
- refine backlog continuously

### 3. Workflow Complexity
Enterprise workflows can become too complex if every edge case is built into the first release.

**Potential impact**
- difficult user experience
- reduced adoption
- longer training time
- slower delivery

**Mitigation ideas**
- prioritize core workflow needs first
- reduce unnecessary steps
- design around the most common use cases
- phase in complexity over time

### 4. Limited Adoption
Even a well-designed product may fail if users do not understand its value or if the workflow feels harder than current methods.

**Potential impact**
- inconsistent use
- incomplete data
- parallel manual processes
- limited ROI

**Mitigation ideas**
- improve usability
- communicate product value clearly
- support onboarding and training
- monitor feedback after launch

### 5. Insufficient Visibility Into Bottlenecks
If metrics and reporting are weak, teams may not see where work is slowing down.

**Potential impact**
- unresolved delays
- poor prioritization decisions
- slow improvement cycles

**Mitigation ideas**
- define metrics early
- track cycle time and aging items
- monitor workflow stages consistently
- review reporting regularly

### 6. Change in Priorities
Enterprise priorities may shift because of leadership changes, risk concerns, funding decisions, or new business needs.

**Potential impact**
- roadmap changes
- delayed releases
- reprioritized backlog
- incomplete initiatives

**Mitigation ideas**
- keep roadmap flexible
- document tradeoffs
- revisit priorities on a regular cadence
- separate MVP from future enhancements

### 7. Tool or Integration Constraints
The product may depend on existing enterprise tools, workflow systems, or reporting environments.

**Potential impact**
- design limitations
- delayed implementation
- inconsistent user experience across systems

**Mitigation ideas**
- identify technical constraints early
- design with current-state limitations in mind
- reduce dependency on custom work where possible
- plan phased enhancements

### 8. Governance and Control Requirements
Risk-sensitive environments often require reviews, controls, approvals, and documentation that can slow delivery if not designed well.

**Potential impact**
- longer cycle time
- additional workflow complexity
- reduced agility

**Mitigation ideas**
- involve governance partners early
- design workflows that balance controls and usability
- define required approvals clearly
- document decision points and rationale

## Key Dependencies

### 1. Stakeholder Participation
The product depends on ongoing input from business, risk, operations, governance, and technology stakeholders.

**Why it matters**
- supports accurate requirements
- improves adoption
- helps prioritize the right work

### 2. Product and Process Ownership
A clear owner or ownership model is needed to make decisions, manage backlog priorities, and resolve tradeoffs.

**Why it matters**
- reduces ambiguity
- accelerates decisions
- improves accountability

### 3. Defined Workflow Stages
The product depends on agreement about major workflow stages, statuses, and handoff points.

**Why it matters**
- supports consistency
- enables status tracking
- improves reporting clarity

### 4. Data Quality at Intake
Accurate workflow execution depends on complete and reliable request information from the start.

**Why it matters**
- reduces rework
- improves review quality
- supports better prioritization

### 5. Reporting and Measurement Capability
The product depends on the ability to measure workflow health through dashboards, reports, or operational metrics.

**Why it matters**
- supports continuous improvement
- helps identify bottlenecks
- enables KPI/KRI tracking

### 6. Existing Systems and Platforms
The workflow may depend on enterprise tools already in place for intake, reporting, case management, or automation.

**Why it matters**
- influences design choices
- affects scalability
- shapes implementation options

### 7. Delivery Capacity
The roadmap depends on available team capacity across product, design, operations, and technology support.

**Why it matters**
- affects timeline realism
- shapes MVP scope
- determines sequencing of enhancements

## Risk Review Questions

A strong delivery team should revisit questions such as:

- Are stakeholders aligned on goals and scope?
- Are requirements clear enough to support delivery?
- Is the workflow too complex for an MVP?
- Where are the main adoption risks?
- Which dependencies are most likely to delay progress?
- What should be simplified, phased, or deferred?

## Summary

A strong product approach recognizes that risks and dependencies are not separate from delivery—they are part of delivery. Identifying them early helps teams make better roadmap decisions, design more usable workflows, and improve the chances of successful adoption.
